# Contents

## A Book Unlike Any Other

The Qur'an occupies a singular position in the history of human civilisation: it is the most memorised text on earth, recited daily by over a billion people in its original Arabic, {{The Arabic of the Qur'an belongs to a prestige register of 7th-century Hijazi dialect, yet remains the liturgical standard. No other scripture is recited exclusively in its revealed tongue across such a geographic span.}} unchanged since its revelation to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ between 610 and 632 CE.

This is not a confessional claim alone. It is a historical one — and it is one that secular and non-Muslim scholars, examining the manuscript and oral evidence, have been compelled to affirm.

---

## Two Parallel Channels of Transmission

The Qur'an was preserved through two simultaneous and mutually correcting channels:

### 1. Oral Memorisation — *Ḥifẓ*

From the moment of revelation, the Prophet ﷺ recited each verse to his Companions, who committed them to memory. {{The Arabic root ح-ف-ظ (*ḥ-f-ẓ*) means "to guard, to preserve." A *ḥāfiẓ* (plural: *ḥuffāẓ*) is one who guards the Qur'an in the chest. The same root is used for God's own attribute of *al-Ḥafīẓ* — the Preserver.}} The Companions who had memorised the complete text were known as *ḥuffāẓ*. The Prophet himself would revise the entire revealed corpus with the Angel Jibrīl once every Ramadan, and twice in the final year of his life — a narration preserved in Bukhārī. {{Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, Kitāb Faḍā'il al-Qur'ān, no. 4998. This annual *ʿarḍ* (presentation) served as a canonical checkpoint across the community, not merely a private recitation.}}

This oral channel has never broken. Today there are an estimated **ten million** people alive who have memorised the entire Qur'an — 6,236 verses, 77,000+ words — to the letter, including precise rules of recitation (*tajwīd*) governing every vowel, pause, and articulation point.

### 2. Written Compilation — *Jam' al-Qur'ān*

Alongside memorisation, verses were written down during the Prophet's lifetime on whatever was available: {{*Riqa'* (parchment scraps), *lakhāf* (flat stones), *aktāf* (shoulder blades), *ʿasīb* (palm stalks). The Arabs of the Hijaz were not a literate civilisation by default, yet revelation created a scribe class. Among the Companions designated as scribes were Zayd ibn Thābit, Ubayy ibn Ka'b, ʿAbdullāh ibn Masʿūd, and Muʿāwiya ibn Abī Sufyān.}} parchment, bone, and palm leaves.

<div style={hadithStyle}>
  "Collect the Qur'an for me, for I fear the loss of those who have memorised it in the battles."
  <br /><br />
  — Umar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb to Abū Bakr al-Ṣiddīq, following the Battle of Yamāma (632 CE), in which seventy *ḥuffāẓ* were martyred. Narrated in Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, no. 4986.
</div>

Under Abū Bakr, Zayd ibn Thābit led a commission that compiled the revelations into a single *muṣḥaf* (codex), accepting nothing without two witnesses and cross-verification against the memorised text. {{Zayd's methodology is notable. He reportedly said: "By God, if they had asked me to move a mountain it would not have been heavier than what they asked of me — to compile the Qur'an." He verified every verse against at least two independent witnesses to prevent any single point of failure. Ibn Ḥajar, *Fatḥ al-Bārī*, vol. 9, p. 11.}} Under ʿUthmān ibn ʿAffān (644–656 CE), five authorised copies were made and distributed to the major garrison cities of the expanding empire, with a recitation master (*qāriʾ*) sent alongside each copy — ensuring the written and oral channels remained locked together.

## Orientalist Testimonies

What is remarkable is the degree to which scholars who arrived with sceptical or hostile intent found themselves affirming the integrity of the text.

<div style={hadithStyle}>
  "The Koran is an essentially authentic reproduction of Muhammad's revelations."
  <br /><br />
  — William Montgomery Watt, *Muhammad at Mecca* (1953), p. 57. Watt was a Church of Scotland minister and among the most respected Western biographers of the Prophet.
</div>

<div style={hadithStyle}>
  "There is probably no other book in the world that has remained twelve centuries with so pure a text."
  <br /><br />
  — Sir William Muir, *The Life of Mahomet* (1861), vol. 1, p. xxvii. Muir was among the most critical Western biographers of the Prophet, yet could not contest the textual integrity of the Qur'an.
</div>

Kenneth Cragg, the Anglican bishop and Arabist, {{Kenneth Cragg (1913–2012), Bishop of Cairo and later Assistant Bishop of Chichester, spent decades studying Islam from within Muslim communities. His *The Mind of the Qur'an* (1973) remains a landmark of sympathetic critical engagement.}} wrote: "The Qur'an, in terms of its text, is in as close proximity to its origin as any book of antiquity." This is not a concession — it is a finding that emerges from the same philological methodology applied to Greek, Hebrew, and Latin texts.

## The Internal Claim and Its Implications

The Qur'an itself issues a direct, falsifiable challenge:

<div style={hadithStyle}>
  *"Indeed, it is We who sent down the Reminder, and indeed, it is We who are its Guardian."*
  <br /><br />
  — Qur'an 15:9 (Al-Ḥijr)
</div>

This verse, revealed in Mecca, constitutes a divine guarantee of preservation. {{Verse 15:9 is often cited in Islamic theology as the textual basis for the doctrine of *ḥifẓ* (divine preservation). Theologians note the deliberate use of *naḥnu* (We, the royal/majestic plural) and the active present tense *laḥāfiẓūn* — "We are indeed its Guardians" — indicating an ongoing, not merely past, protection.}} The claim is not merely theological — it is empirically testable across fourteen centuries of textual history. The fact that no variant recension has emerged, that memorised and written traditions confirm each other across continents and centuries, and that even adversarial scholarship has failed to produce a competing text — all of this coheres with the claim.

---

## A Living Tradition

The preservation of the Qur'an is not a museum piece. It is an active, living practice. In *kuttāb* schools from Mauritania to Malaysia, children memorise the entire text before secondary school. The *Tarawīḥ* prayers of Ramadan cover the full Qur'an in thirty nights before congregations of thousands. Every recitation is governed by an *isnād* — a chain of transmission — that stretches back, teacher to student, to the Prophet ﷺ.

This is unprecedented in the history of scripture. {{Compare: the earliest complete manuscripts of the New Testament (Codex Sinaiticus, Codex Vaticanus) date to the 4th century CE — over 300 years after Jesus. The Old Testament's earliest extensive witness is the Dead Sea Scrolls (c. 150 BCE–70 CE), still centuries after composition. The Qur'an's earliest manuscripts date to within decades of revelation, with living oral chains that have never been interrupted.}}

The book you hold — or recite — is the same book that was revealed. That is not a miracle claimed in isolation. It is a historical fact.
